# Design 90: Wayfinding & Information Architecture

**Category:** Data & Visualization  
**Style Tags:** wayfinding, signage, information-architecture, London-Underground, clear, system  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Best For:** Organizational structure presentations, system map communications, onboarding journey maps, process architecture documentation, and any content where helping an audience navigate a complex information space is the primary challenge. Ideal for information architects, UX designers, and organizational designers.

---

## Visual Direction

London Underground map by Harry Beck, airport wayfinding signage systems, hospital navigation design, and information architecture diagram conventions — the design tradition of making complex navigation systems simple through systematic visual encoding. Color = category. Shape = type. Size = importance. Consistent and learnable at a glance.

## Background & Texture

Clean white (#FFFFFF) — wayfinding systems use white backgrounds to maximize contrast with the systematic color encoding. Transport for London uses this standard and it has become universal for wayfinding clarity.

## Typography

Johnston-style or equivalent clear transport sans-serif (Univers, DIN, or the specific TfL Johnston typeface). Bold for primary labels. Regular for secondary. All-caps for zone and category labels. Highly legible at small sizes since wayfinding must work at distance and speed.

## Color Palette

- **Primary:** Wayfinding blue (#003087) — structural elements, primary navigation
- **Secondary:** Clean white (#FFFFFF) — background surface
- **Accent 1:** Zone red (#CC2200) — urgent, exit, critical path elements
- **Accent 2:** Zone green (#059669) — confirmed path, completed zones, safe areas
- **Neutral:** Zone gray (#6B7280) — inactive elements, context, secondary zones

## Layout & Composition

Diagrammatic rather than geographic — elements are positioned for diagram readability rather than spatial accuracy (the Harry Beck innovation). Station/node dots at regular grid intersections. Lines at 0°, 45°, or 90° angles only. Interchanges shown as rings around multi-line nodes. Zone boundaries shown as concentric circles or zone label areas.

## Graphic Elements & Decorations

Colored line segments representing categories or pathways. Circular dot nodes at stops/stages. Interchange rings at multi-line connections. Zone concentric labels. Directional arrows at terminal points. Legend showing line colors and names. Grid snap for all elements (0°/45°/90° only). Status indicators for stations (open circle = active, filled = terminus, ring = interchange).

## Slide Types & Templates

- **Title Slide:** System map as the hero image — all lines and nodes — with the network name in transport design typography above.
- **Content Slide:** Zoomed view of one section of the network, with the selected zone highlighted and other zones in gray.
- **Process/Flow Slide:** Journey map on the network — highlighted path from origin to destination through multiple interchange nodes, with steps annotated.
- **Data/Chart Slide:** System statistics displayed in transport design aesthetic — ridership, line counts, interchange nodes — as a clear information panel.
- **Closing Slide:** Full system map with all lines and nodes — the complete view of the information architecture.

## Tone & Mood

Systematically clear, reassuringly organized, and navigably confident. The tone communicates: this system has been understood and organized for your use. There is nothing uncertain in a well-designed wayfinding system — it tells you exactly where you are and how to get where you need to go. Audiences feel oriented and confident.

## Avoid

- Geographically accurate but diagram-illegible layouts
- Irregular angles beyond 0°/45°/90°
- Too many colors (one per line/category maximum)
- Crossing lines without interchange rings
- Missing direction indicators
- Any element that creates navigational ambiguity

## Example Prompt

> Design a presentation slide as a transit-style wayfinding and information architecture map. Use a clean white background, five colored lines at 0°/45°/90° angles only (blue, red, green, yellow, purple), circular station nodes at regular grid intersections along each line, double-ring interchange nodes where multiple lines connect, station/stage labels in clean Johnston-style sans-serif positioned consistently above or below each node, zone concentric circle boundaries in the background, directional terminus arrows at each line's end, a compact legend panel in the lower-left, and the system or organization name in transport design typography above. The feel should be systematically clear, diagrammatically precise, and wayfinding-confident — London Underground aesthetics applied to information architecture.
