# Design 82: Infographic Illustrated

**Category:** Data & Visualization  
**Style Tags:** infographic, illustrated, icons, narrative-data, accessible, shareable  
**Difficulty:** Beginner  
**Best For:** Public-facing data communications, social media content, annual impact reports for non-expert audiences, awareness campaign materials, and any context where making complex data visually engaging and immediately comprehensible to a wide audience is the goal. Ideal for communications teams, NGOs, and public educators.

---

## Visual Direction

Bloomberg Visual Data, National Geographic data visualization, and Vox Explainer aesthetics — illustrated data with large clear numbers, icon-driven quantification, narrative flow from left to right, a single central thesis supported by multiple visual evidence streams, and the specific quality of "designed for understanding" rather than designed for showing off.

## Background & Texture

Clean white (#FFFFFF) or very light warm gray (#F8F8F6) — the infographic tradition prioritizes maximum signal-to-noise ratio. No decorative texture. Color comes from the data elements themselves, not from background decoration.

## Typography

Clean, friendly, bold sans-serif — Montserrat, Poppins, or similar that reads well at all sizes. Very large numbers for key statistics (the "hero number"). Medium bold for labeled categories. Small regular weight for explanatory notes. The typographic hierarchy must be very clear: number → label → explanation.

## Color Palette

- **Primary:** Clean white (#FFFFFF) — background
- **Secondary:** Deep charcoal (#2A2A2A) — primary text and structural elements
- **Accent 1:** Vivid primary color (blue #2563EB, green #059669, or orange #EA580C) — primary data elements
- **Accent 2:** Complementary secondary (selected to contrast with primary)
- **Neutral:** Light gray (#E5E7EB) — secondary data, background fills, dividers

## Layout & Composition

Top-to-bottom narrative flow: headline statistic or finding first, supporting evidence in visual tiers below. Large illustrated icon paired with number for each key statistic. Two or three column grid for comparison data. Icon matrix for per-capita or proportional data (10 icons with 3 highlighted = 30%). Circular proportion charts. Timeline with milestone markers.

## Graphic Elements & Decorations

Icon sets representing data categories (people icons for demographics, house icons for housing, tree icons for environmental data, etc.). Large bold numbers as primary visual anchors. Simple illustrated graphics that reinforce the data narrative. Proportion visualizations (isotype charts — rows of icons). Progress bar style completion meters. Circular data rings.

## Slide Types & Templates

- **Title Slide:** Infographic headline — the single most important finding in large bold type, icon representing the topic, subheadline with context.
- **Content Slide:** Three or four key statistics as bold number + icon + label combinations in a grid, supporting bar chart below.
- **Process/Flow Slide:** Left-to-right narrative arc — "Here is the problem → Here is the scale → Here is the solution → Here is the evidence."
- **Data/Chart Slide:** Primary chart with full visual treatment — labeled bars, icons, trend arrows, bold callout for the key finding.
- **Closing Slide:** "What this means" synthesis — top 3 takeaways as bold visual summary cards with icons.

## Tone & Mood

Accessible, visually engaging, and democratically informative. The tone communicates: this data matters to you, even if you're not a data expert, and we've made it possible to understand in 30 seconds. There is respect for the audience's intelligence alongside respect for their time. Audiences feel informed rather than overwhelmed.

## Avoid

- Complex chart types requiring expert reading
- Small text or dense data layouts
- Multiple competing visual hierarchies
- Chartjunk that creates visual noise without information value
- Abstract geometric decoration without data meaning
- Any element that makes the data feel intimidating

## Example Prompt

> Design a presentation slide as a data infographic. Use a clean white background, one large bold "hero number" statistic with a relevant icon above it, three supporting statistics as icon + number + label combinations in a row below, a simple illustrated bar chart showing comparison data, two icons representing key data categories at large scale, bold data labels directly on chart elements, a warm color palette (one primary vivid blue or green, one complementary accent), and a brief explanatory caption below the chart. The feel should be accessible, visually engaging, and immediately informative — infographic design for wide audiences.
