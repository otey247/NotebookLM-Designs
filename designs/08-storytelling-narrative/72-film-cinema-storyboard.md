# Design 72: Film & Cinema Storyboard

**Category:** Storytelling & Narrative  
**Style Tags:** storyboard, film, screenplay, Hitchcock, cinematic, frame-by-frame  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Best For:** Video production planning, marketing campaign creative briefs, brand storytelling, experience design documentation, and any content that benefits from visual sequence planning. Ideal for creative directors, video producers, and storytellers planning visual content.

---

## Visual Direction

Alfred Hitchcock storyboard aesthetics, traditional animator's exposure sheets, director's cut sequence planning, and the visual vocabulary of film production. Storyboard frames with shot composition notations, camera movement arrows, dialogue and action lines, scene and shot numbering, and the working-document feel of real production materials.

## Background & Texture

Off-white storyboard paper (#F5F0E8) — the color of professional storyboard template pads. A grid of storyboard frame rectangles (in the standard 16:9 or 4:3 aspect ratio) with light gray border lines. The background is a working storyboard template, pre-printed and ready to fill in.

## Typography

Typewriter or handwritten-style font for production notes, dialogue, and annotations — the specific font of a working film production document. Bold for scene numbers and shot types. Italic for technical camera notes. Action description in present-tense screenplay format (SARAH ENTERS THE ROOM. SHE LOOKS UP.).

## Color Palette

- **Primary:** Storyboard paper white (#F5F0E8) — background surface
- **Secondary:** Pencil gray (#888888) — frame borders, sketch marks
- **Accent 1:** Director's blue (#1A3A6A) — camera move annotations, director's notes
- **Accent 2:** Actor/action red (#CC2200) — actor movements, blocking marks
- **Neutral:** Typewriter black (#2A2A2A) — all text and final notation elements

## Layout & Composition

Standard storyboard grid: six to nine frames arranged in two or three rows, each frame with dialogue/description space below. Shot type, angle, and movement noted beside each frame. Scene and shot number in the upper-left corner of each frame. Camera move arrows within frames. Page number in the upper-right corner of the board.

## Graphic Elements & Decorations

Storyboard frame borders (thick enough to clearly define each shot). Camera angle arrows (→ pan, ↑ tilt, ↕ dolly). Movement arrows showing actor blocking within frames. Scene number labels (SC. 12, SHOT 4). Shot type abbreviations (CU, MS, LS, ECU, POV, OTS). "INT/EXT" and location labels. Rough sketched stick-figure compositions showing camera placement. Continuity marks showing match-on-action relationships.

## Slide Types & Templates

- **Title Slide:** Storyboard format header with production title, director's name, date, revision number, and production company/client name. One large frame as the title visual.
- **Content Slide:** 6-frame storyboard grid with rough shot compositions, shot type labels, dialogue text below each frame, camera movement annotations.
- **Process/Flow Slide:** Sequential storyboard reading as a process narrative — each frame a process step rendered as a "scene."
- **Data/Chart Slide:** Key data visualized as one "frame" in the storyboard with director's commentary in the adjacent description field.
- **Closing Slide:** "THE END" frame with credits styled as a film closing scroll, production information in typewriter text.

## Tone & Mood

Creative-process-transparent, visual-narrative, and productively collaborative. The tone communicates: here is how we are visualizing this story, and here is where your input can shape it. The working-document aesthetic invites iteration and collaboration. Audiences feel they are participants in the creative process.

## Avoid

- Polished or final-look photography (storyboards are working sketches)
- Complete written scripts (use brief action descriptions only)
- Multiple typeface families
- Decorative elements outside the storyboard vocabulary
- Final-design aesthetics
- Anything that discourages iteration and marks the work as "done"

## Example Prompt

> Design a presentation slide as a film production storyboard. Use a storyboard paper off-white background with a 6-frame grid in two rows of three, each frame bordered in pencil gray with a 16:9 aspect ratio. Include rough sketch compositions in each frame, shot type labels (CU, MS, ECU) in the upper-left corner of each frame, scene/shot numbering (SC. 01 SHOT A), camera movement arrows within frames, brief action description in typewriter font below each frame, actor blocking marks in red, and director's camera notes in blue. Include a production header with project title, director, and revision date. The feel should be working-document authentic, visually narrative, and collaboratively open — film storyboard as presentation format.
