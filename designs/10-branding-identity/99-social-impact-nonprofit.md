# Design 99: Social Impact & Nonprofit

**Category:** Branding & Identity  
**Style Tags:** nonprofit, social-impact, advocacy, human-centered, community, purpose  
**Difficulty:** Beginner  
**Best For:** Nonprofit communications, impact report presentations, advocacy campaign materials, donor cultivation pitches, community program briefings, and any context where communicating organizational purpose and human impact creates more trust than communicating products or services. Ideal for nonprofit leaders, development officers, and social sector communicators.

---

## Visual Direction

Gates Foundation impact report aesthetics, Doctors Without Borders/MSF field communication, global development organization storytelling — human photography at its most authentic, impact statistics made human through stories, a color palette that communicates warmth and action simultaneously, and the specific credibility of organizations that do important work and need others to know it.

## Background & Texture

Warm, clean white (#FFFFFF) or warm light beige (#FFF8F0) — the social sector communicates with warmth and approachability. Occasional deep teal (#006B6B) or warm orange (#E87722) for impact section backgrounds. No corporate texture — authentic human texture comes from the photography.

## Typography

Warm, humanist sans-serif — Nunito, Source Sans, or Lato. Bold for impact statistics and mission statements. Light for supporting narrative. The typography should feel human and approachable — never cold or clinical. Impact numbers in bold at large scale.

## Color Palette

- **Primary:** Impact teal (#006B6B) — purpose, action, hope
- **Secondary:** Warm white (#FFFBF5) — warmth and approachability
- **Accent 1:** Action orange (#E87722) — energy, urgency, call to action
- **Accent 2:** Warm human skin tones — from the photography
- **Neutral:** Warm gray (#6B6560) — supporting text, secondary information

## Layout & Composition

Human photography as the primary content element — authentic photographs of program participants, staff, and communities in real contexts. Impact statistics alongside or within the photography zone. Story-first structure: individual human story → statistics that contextualize the scale → organizational response. The person's face carries more weight than any data point.

## Graphic Elements & Decorations

Impact statistic callout boxes ("10,000 children served in 2024"). Testimonial quote with person's photograph and name. Program reach map. Funding accountability chart (where each dollar goes). Progress toward goal meter. Partner logos. "Before / After" community photography pairs. Volunteer and staff headshots in circles.

## Slide Types & Templates

- **Title Slide:** Authentic community photograph, organization name and mission statement, year and program name.
- **Content Slide:** Human story lead — individual photograph with name and story — followed by the scale context statistic.
- **Process/Flow Slide:** Theory of change arc — needs assessment → program intervention → short-term outcomes → long-term impact.
- **Data/Chart Slide:** Impact report visualization — beneficiaries served, geographic reach, program outcomes, with human photographs alongside every statistic.
- **Closing Slide:** Impact summary and call to action — donate, volunteer, partner — with authentic community image.

## Tone & Mood

Purposefully human, accountably transparent, and optimistically urgent. The tone communicates: this work matters, it's working, and more is possible with your support. There is authentic warmth that comes from proximity to real human impact. Audiences feel the genuine importance of the work and their role in it.

## Avoid

- Stock photography of generic volunteers or beneficiaries
- Impact statistics without human context
- Corporate or cold visual language
- Overselling success without honest acknowledgment of ongoing challenges
- Imagery that exploits suffering rather than celebrating dignity
- Missing authentic community voice (people should tell their own stories)

## Example Prompt

> Design a presentation slide in social impact nonprofit style. Use a warm white background, an authentic community photography placeholder in the left 50% showing a real program context (warm, dignified, hopeful), three large bold impact statistics in teal (#006B6B) on the right ("12,847 lives changed", "3 countries", "2024 impact"), below the statistics: a brief testimonial from a program participant in italic quotation format with their name, a warm orange action button ("Support our work"), and the organization's name and logo in the upper left. The feel should be purposefully human, accountably transparent, and optimistically urgent — social impact nonprofit aesthetics that communicate genuine human change.
