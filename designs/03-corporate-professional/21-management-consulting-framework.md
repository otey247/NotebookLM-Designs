# Design 21: Management Consulting Framework

**Category:** Corporate & Professional  
**Style Tags:** consulting, McKinsey, framework, analytical, pyramid-principle, executive  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Best For:** Strategic recommendations, executive briefings, business transformation proposals, and any presentation where a structured analytical argument needs to be made to a senior business audience. Ideal for strategy consultants, business analysts, and executive teams.

---

## Visual Direction

McKinsey, BCG, and Bain aesthetics — the visual language of top-tier management consulting. The design communicates structured thinking, analytical rigor, and executive-level confidence. Every slide answers "so what?" with visual as much as verbal clarity. Frameworks (2×2 matrices, issue trees, pyramid principle) are first-class visual elements.

## Background & Texture

Clean white (#FFFFFF) with no decorative texture. Professional and undistracting — the analysis is the focus. Light gray (#F5F5F5) section header bars and background zones provide structure without distraction.

## Typography

Clean, neutral sans-serif — Arial, Calibri, or a modern alternative like Inter or Source Sans. Consistent typographic hierarchy: bold 18–20pt headlines, regular 12pt body, 10pt footnotes. Type sizes are deliberate and consistent — no random size variations. Headlines in navy or dark text, body in charcoal.

## Color Palette

- **Primary:** Deep navy (#1A2A4A) — headlines, key data, structural elements
- **Secondary:** Clean white (#FFFFFF) — background
- **Accent 1:** Consulting teal (#006B6B) — positive findings, opportunity callouts
- **Accent 2:** Alert red (#CC3333) — risks, critical findings, negative trends
- **Neutral:** Slate gray (#6B7280) — secondary data, supporting text, grid lines

## Layout & Composition

Maximum information density within a clear visual hierarchy. Title at the top as the "so what" statement. Body of the slide contains supporting evidence, frameworks, and data. Bottom third often carries detailed supporting data or footnotes. Column-based layouts (one or two columns) for comparing options. Consistent page margins (0.5 inches equivalent) maintained throughout.

## Graphic Elements & Decorations

2×2 matrices with labeled quadrants and axes. Pyramid structures for hierarchical arguments. Issue tree or decision tree branching diagrams. Horizontal bar charts with category labels. Simple tables with alternating row shading. Traffic light status indicators (green/amber/red circles). Small numbered footnotes. Client logo placeholder at the top right.

## Slide Types & Templates

- **Title Slide:** Firm logo zone, presentation title in bold navy, client and date line, confidentiality notice at the bottom.
- **Content Slide:** Bold headline as insight statement, 2–3 supporting bullet points, one primary visual (chart, matrix, or diagram), footnote references.
- **Process/Flow Slide:** Swimlane or waterfall diagram showing work phases, timeline milestones, ownership RACI indicators.
- **Data/Chart Slide:** Primary chart at 60% of slide, insight box with bold finding highlighted, supporting data table, data source footnote.
- **Closing Slide:** Summary of top 3 recommendations, next steps table with owner/timeline/priority, appendix reference note.

## Tone & Mood

Analytically rigorous, executive-ready, and confidently direct. The tone says: this analysis was done carefully, the conclusions are defensible, and the recommendations are actionable. There is no hedging in the visual language — every element communicates structured confidence.

## Avoid

- Decorative colors or visual elements without analytical function
- Vague headlines (use insight statements: "X is the key driver of Y" not "Key Drivers")
- Overly complex charts that require minutes to understand
- Inconsistent typography or spacing
- Stock photography (use diagrams, frameworks, and data instead)
- Casual or informal visual language

## Example Prompt

> Design a presentation slide in management consulting style inspired by McKinsey and BCG presentations. Use a clean white background, deep navy as the primary color, a bold headline in the consulting style (written as an insight statement rather than a topic), a 2×2 matrix with labeled quadrants and axes as the primary visual, supporting bullet points in clean Arial or Inter, a traffic light status indicator row, and a data source footnote at the bottom. Include a client logo placeholder at the top right. The feel should be analytically rigorous, executive-facing, and confidently direct — the visual language of structured business thinking.
