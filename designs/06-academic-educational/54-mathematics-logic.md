# Design 54: Mathematics & Formal Logic

**Category:** Academic & Educational  
**Style Tags:** mathematics, formal-logic, LaTeX, proof, notation, rigorous  
**Difficulty:** Advanced  
**Best For:** Mathematics education presentations, formal methods documentation, algorithmic thinking workshops, computational science briefings, and any presentation where mathematical rigor and symbolic precision are the primary communication goals. Ideal for mathematicians, computer scientists, and logic instructors.

---

## Visual Direction

LaTeX typesetting aesthetics applied to presentation design — the specific visual language of mathematical publishing: theorem-lemma-corollary structure, proof blocks, equation-forward slides, number lines, and the quiet authority of mathematical notation. The design communicates: this argument is formal, complete, and provable.

## Background & Texture

Clean white (#FFFFFF) or very light gray (#F8F8F8) — the LaTeX article class default. Graph paper background as a subtle texture option for geometry-heavy content. No decorative surface. The equations and notation ARE the design.

## Typography

LaTeX-inspired: Computer Modern Serif (or a close equivalent like Latin Modern) for all mathematical content. Body text in the same serif family. Equations typeset in proper mathematical notation with italic variables, upright operators, and correct spacing. Bold for theorem statements. Italic for variable names in prose. Small-cap for theorem and definition labels (THEOREM 1, DEFINITION 3).

## Color Palette

- **Primary:** Clean white (#FFFFFF) — background surface
- **Secondary:** LaTeX black (#1A1A1A) — primary notation and text
- **Accent 1:** Definition blue (#1E5FA8) — highlighted definition boxes
- **Accent 2:** Theorem red (#9B1A1A) — theorem and key result boxes
- **Neutral:** Proof green (#1A6B35) — proof environment boxes, QED marks

## Layout & Composition

Mathematical paper structure: definition → theorem → proof → corollary flow. Wide equation display zone in the center of the slide. Proof steps numbered and indented. QED square (□ or ∎) at the right margin of proof conclusions. Numbered equations referenced in text (equation 3.2). Commutative diagrams for algebraic structures. Number line or coordinate system as visual anchor for geometric content.

## Graphic Elements & Decorations

Theorem environment boxes (bordered rectangles with "Theorem N" bold label). Definition boxes in blue background. Proof blocks with "Proof:" in italic and ∎ at the end. LaTeX equation display blocks (centered, numbered, with display-style spacing). Commutative diagram arrows. Venn diagram for set theory. Coordinate axes for function plots. Mathematical table (truth table, multiplication table, matrix).

## Slide Types & Templates

- **Title Slide:** Paper title in LaTeX article style, authors with institutional affiliations, abstract, subject classification codes (MSC 2010 or AMS codes).
- **Content Slide:** Section heading (§3. Main Results), definition box, lemma or theorem statement, followed by proof sketch.
- **Process/Flow Slide:** Proof architecture diagram — main theorem at top, required lemmas below, proof strategy connecting them with logical dependency arrows.
- **Data/Chart Slide:** Mathematical visualization — function plot with labeled axes and asymptotes, or truth table, or formal language automaton diagram.
- **Closing Slide:** Open problems section, acknowledgments, bibliography in LaTeX/BibTeX style.

## Tone & Mood

Rigorously precise, intellectually demanding, and formally complete. The tone communicates that every statement is carefully made, every claim is provable, and every definition is exact. There is a specific kind of intellectual beauty in mathematical precision that rewards the careful reader.

## Avoid

- Approximate language or hedged claims (mathematics is precise)
- Decorative visual elements without mathematical function
- Sans-serif for mathematical notation (LaTeX conventions use serif)
- Missing proof steps or unsupported assertions
- Informal presentation of formal content
- Any element that could not appear in a published mathematical paper

## Example Prompt

> Design a presentation slide in mathematical and formal logic style inspired by LaTeX typesetting conventions. Use a clean white background, Computer Modern-style serif typography for all text, a bold-bordered "THEOREM 1" box with the theorem statement in standard mathematical notation, a "Proof:" block below with numbered proof steps and the QED mark (∎) at the right margin of the final step, a displayed equation block centered on the slide with equation number at the right margin, a definition box with light blue background containing a formal definition with bold variable names in proper LaTeX italic style, and a commutative diagram or truth table as a supporting figure. The feel should be formally rigorous, mathematically precise, and quietly authoritative.
